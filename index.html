Gotta Class 'em All!¶

Predicting Pokémon Types with Classification Algorithms¶

A data science tutorial by Dionel Olo¶

Why study data science?¶

Scientists collect and analyze decades of data in order to seek out trends or search for correlations. Businesses look at previous customers, trying to predict how best to attract the next ones. Non-profit organizations study the patterns of their previous donators in order to find their most engaged members, and ask them to upgrade or renew their memberships. And sometimes, curious people with a lot of time on their hands just want to find out the answer to an interesting question. All of these are just a few examples of data science, the practice of gathering, processing, and analyzing data in order to see patterns or gain insight on a real-world problem. This webpage will provide an example of a data science study, in which we will use a classification algorithm to sort data into categories.

For our example, we'll be looking at data from the Pokmon video games. Each of the nearly 900 species of Pokmon has up to two types, up to three abilities, a pool of possible moves, and six base stats: HP, Attack, Defense, Special Attack, Special Defense, and Speed. The eighteen types of Pokemon battle each other based on weaknesses and resistances, and their base stats intuitively match their natures. For example, Rock-type Pokmon tend to be defensive and slow, while Electric type Pokmon tend to be fast and frail. But how well does this pattern hold up? How accurately can we predict the type of a Pokmon from its stats? This is the question that we seek to answer using data science. Although any Pokmon player can check the type in-game, and probably knows types better than stats, our question isn't based on a necessity to predict the type. Instead, we are looking to see if a pattern exists.

Collecting our data¶

The data science pipeline is a process which takes raw data and returns some insight on the real-world process which the data describes. This process begins with collecting the data. This step can involve taking measurements, conducting surveys, reading archives, or many other methods to gather information about some topic. For our example, we will be taking the data from Bulbapedia, an online wiki which claims to be "one of the largest Pokmon resources on the internet." Bulbapedia hosts dozens of lists, sorting all Pokmon species by various categories; we are interested in the lists of Pokmon by base stats and by National Pokdex number, which includes their types.

Data cleanup¶

We've collected all the data that we need, but it's currently in two separate tables. We need to merge them.

In [5]:
# Each Pokmon has a unique number in the National Pokdex, which is included in both charts.
# For this study, we will not be considering alternate forms. Dropping duplicate Pokdex numbers helps us both to
# merge the tables, and removes alternate forms.
df_stats = df_stats.drop_duplicates(subset='#', ignore_index = True)
df_types = df_types.drop_duplicates(subset='Ndex', ignore_index = True)

We also need to melt together the types, so a Pokemon with two types will be counted for both. For example, Bulbasaur will have an entry listing it as a Grass type, and a second entry listing it as a Poison type. This will help us look at all Pokmon of a certain type, without relying on the arbitrary type ordering used by the games. One example of this is the Steel/Flying Skarmory and the Flying/Steel Corviknight. Both of these Pokmon are birds known for their high defenses. Under this model, they would be treated as having the same type, and contributing to the averages and data for both Steel and Flying-type Pokmon.

df_types = df_types.melt(id_vars=['Ndex','Pokmon'], value_vars=['Type','Type.1'])

Exploratory Data Analysis¶

The next part of the data science process is exploring the data, looking for initial patterns. Here, we want to see if a correlation exists between stats and type. Pandas comes with several types of plotting functions. Here, we will be grouping together all Pokmon by type, and creating box plots of their base stat total. We want to see if some types tend to have higher BSTs than others.

In [10]:
df.boxplot(column='Total', by=['Type'], figsize = (16,9), grid = False)
Out[10]:
<AxesSubplot:title={'center':'Total'}, xlabel='[Type]'>

Here, we can see that some types tend to have a higher BST than others. Bug type Pokmon, on average, are the weakest, with a mean BST of under 400. Dragon type Pokmon are the strongest, with a mean BST of approximately 500. There is only one outlier: by looking at the BSTs of Fighting-type Pokmon, we can determine that this must be Tyrogue, a weak pre-evolution.

We can see that there is some correlation between type and statistics. But what about for the individual stats? Gamers know that intuitively, a Pikachu can run circles around a Geodude, but how well does this hold up numerically? We can use another box plot to find out how types correspond with base speed.

In [11]:
df.boxplot(column='Speed', by=['Type'], figsize = (16,9), grid = False)
Out[11]:
<AxesSubplot:title={'center':'Speed'}, xlabel='[Type]'>

Our intuition is correct: on average, Electric type Pokmon are the fastest in the game, with an average speed of just over 75. Clearly visible on the Electric box plot as an outlier is the blazing fast Regieleki, with an base speed of 200. Befitting their solid stature, Rock and Steel type Pokmon are the slowest on average.

Another question that comes to mind is "How strongly are two base stats correlated"? If these correlations vary between types, then there is evidence that stats are a viable predictor of type. To continue with our examples of Rock and Electric type Pokmon, we will plot their speed against their defense on a scatter plot.

From the slopes of the regression line, as well as the line plotted on the graph, we can see that Rock type Pokmon lose more defense for every point of speed they gain than Electric type Pokmon. The correlation is stronger for Rock types than for Electric types, which indicates that all six stats are correlated with type, rather than just a single stat or the BST.

Machine Learning¶

We've mentioned a classification algorithm once at the start of this tutorial, but now is a great time to get into more detail, before implementing one of our own. Classification algorithms are a class of machine learning algorithm which take in predictor data and output the most likely class that the predictor data belongs to. For this study, the predictor data is the base stats of a Pokmon, and the class is the type. Essentially, the machine "learns" what type a Pokmon with certain stats has, and uses that knowledge to predict the rest.

We will be using scikit-learn's Linear SVM, a support vector machine. This machine works by calculating a hyperplane that divides the set of data based on categories. The support vectors the algorithm is named after are vectors between the hyperplane and its closest points. scikit-learn also provides other tools, such as a tool that generates training and test data.

In [13]:
# Split the data into two halves, for training and for testing.
# Our predictors are the base stats of the Pokmon, and our target is the type.
df_stats_only = df[['HP','Attack','Defense','Sp. Attack','Sp. Defense','Speed','Total']]

# We preprocess the training data so the classification equation is solved faster.
scaler = StandardScaler()
scaler.fit(df_stats_only)

x_train, x_test, y_train, y_test = train_test_split(scaler.transform(df_stats_only), df[['Type']], test_size=0.5)

Here, we can see the first five rows of training data. The data is shuffled, but each row of statistics corresponds with one of the Pokmon's types. The matching index values show that shuffling does not break up matching pairs of data. Now, we need to feed the training data into the SVM.

In [14]:
# Create a linear SVM. We set the maximum iterations and tolerance to ensure convrgence.
linsvm = svm.LinearSVC(max_iter = 10000, tol=1e-6)
In [15]:
# The values of types are in a column array, but the fit() function expects them in a row.
# We must reshape the array.We get the numpy array of values, then use ravel() to reshape it.
linsvm.fit(x_train, y_train.values.ravel())

From these values, the algorithm seems to be inaccurate. But how accurate is the whole thing? We can find out using k-fold cross-validation. With this method, the data is partitioned into k slices, where k - 1 of them are training data, and the remaining slice is test data. The cross-validation function will return an array of k elements, scored as the percent of correct predictions. We can also show the mean and standard deviation of these guesses.

In [17]:
# k-fold cross-validation with 10 partitions.
scores = cross_val_score(linsvm, scaler.transform(df_stats_only), df[['Type']].values.ravel(), cv = 10)

Conclusion and Further Study¶

With this low of an accuracy, we can claim that the base stats of a Pokmon are not a strong predictor of its type. However, this is not necessarily bad. This helps make the gameplay less predictable; a Rock-type user can have Aerodactyl outspeed and land a powerful Earthquake on the fast Electric Pikachu. Fighting-type moves that make defensive Rock and Steel-types crumble do hardly anything to the just as defensive, Water/Poison Toxapex... who still doesn't look forward to Ground-type moves. If every Pokmon type had a clear stat spread, then it would have a clear competitive niche, and the metagame would base itself solely on building with and countering these archetypes. However, with the diversity of 898 species, there's plenty of room to build around your favorite Pokmon, or find the perfect cornerstone to your latest team.

There's more data science that can be done. For example, earlier we noted that Dragon-type Pokmon are, on average, stronger than Bug-type Pokmon. How much of that is because Bug-types are found much earlier in the game than Dragon-types? We could plot each Pokmon's BST against the route it is found on. This would require us to standardize route numbers across games, and collect data from multiple tables. By now, you've learned enough about the data science pipeline to plan out how you would do this project, or any other projects you feel motivated to do.

The world of data science, like the world of Pokmon, is a vast, wonderful, growing, and unpredictable world. With this tutorial, you've taken your first step into the tall grass, and are ready to be the very best data scientist!
